About Dr Fox

It has been my honor to serve as your mayor these past four years. In that time, Burning Man has grown from 50,000 people to more than 65,000. During my tenure, the event sold out for the first time, as it has every time since.

But the position of mayor is not about the event; it’s about the city. It's about the community and the people who raise it out of the dust.

In last year’s State of the City address, I promised that I would continue working hard for you, and I have. We put into effect Our Burning Man Traffic Mitigation Plan, which limited admission to the city to only 35,000 vehicles and requiring an additional fee for each.

But there is more work to be done.

From the Fox's mouth:

What does being mayor of BRC entail?
Legally speaking, the mayor speaks for both the government and the community. As mayor, I have all the powers and duties for the office of council-member in addition to those of mayor, effectively making me part of the Burning Man Organization while holding the post, though they don't agree with me on that point.

What are the biggest challenges of your mayorship?
We are an incredibly diverse city in ways that most cities aren't. I govern according to the Ten Principles, to the best of my ability, but it can be challenging to fairly engage with all extremes of that diversity without compromising on those principles. That's become easier over the years, and I think the record shows that I've done well in this regard.

Why don't more people know about the BRC mayoral election? How do you plan to raise awareness?

The majority of Burners have been coming for three or fewer years. Of the remaining minority, many weren't here during an election year, which is every four years. So only a small fraction have experienced a Black Rock City election and there's simply not much discussion of it during non-election years.

Why should people vote for you?
Not everyone sees Black Rock City as a vibrant, diverse, unique community. Some see it as a party. I fear this will destroy the city and unmake the event as we know it. In the four years of my tenure, we've seen unprecedented growth but not at the expense of our culture. That can be a difficult balance, but under my leadership we've managed it. I don't want us to lose this forward momentum. A vote for me is a vote for the Ten Principles.

What's your hope for the future of Black Rock City?
I hope we can continue moving forward as a community---building art, meeting each other, living with immediacy, gifting, and growing---with me as your mayor. On Friday (before the Saturday of burn night), please remember: vote for Dr Fox.

After serving as mayor of Black Rock City for four years now, I can say that I am proud of what we've accomplished together. I'm running for re-election because there is much more to do, and because there are forces working harder than ever to turn our community into something less than what it could be. To unmake what we have built together.

The Burning Man Org has a lot on their plate, and although Nevada state law requires political representation from population centers such as Black Rock City (see brcelections.org for more information), it's important for the Org to retain the perception of autonomy over the event.

've always been a candidate of principles, as you can see from the way I've governed. I believe that Burning Man can be much more than a party in the desert, but that keeping it that way requires a leader with experience and the will to fight for that vision.
